CHAPTER 1

The Positivist Illusion and Its Undoing: Why History Cannot Claim Objectivity over Fiction

The traditional demarcation between history and literature as exclusive domains has been increasingly destabilized by shifting paradigms regarding truth and reality. Historically, the ascent of positivism in the nineteenth century sought to align historiography with the empirical methodologies of the natural sciences, privileging the “fact” as a verifiable unit of truth and distancing historical writing from literary devices. This professionalization established the historian as a dispassionate seeker of truth, distinguishing the “actual” from the “possible” or “imagined” realms of the novel. By identifying truth solely with fact and regarding fiction as its opposite, positivism framed the imaginative as a hindrance to the understanding of reality rather than a potential way of apprehending it. This rigid binary was not merely a stylistic preference but an epistemological commitment to the idea that the past could be observed with the same clinical detachment as a biological specimen.
To understand the persistence of this demarcation, one must first recognize the profound intellectual necessity that birthed the positivist project. Far from being a mere exercise in narrow-mindedness, the positivist turn represented a vital defensive maneuver against the caprice of myth, hagiography, and state-sponsored propaganda that had long characterized historical accounts. By insisting on the primacy of the verifiable artifact, nineteenth-century scholars sought to establish a democratic and accountable standard of evidence. This methodology provided a crucial safeguard against the distortion of the past by personal whim or romantic fabrication, creating a shared language of inquiry that allowed for the cross-examination of data. In this light, the drive toward professionalization was an ethical imperative to anchor human memory in something more stable than the shifting sands of oral tradition or poetic license. Without this foundational commitment to the “fact” as an external referent, the very possibility of a collective, verifiable reality would remain vulnerable to the totalizing claims of ideology or the erasures of collective amnesia.
However, while this approach provided a structured academic framework for the discipline, it relied upon a foundational illusion: the belief that history represents a static, objective recovery of the past. The unbridgeable gap between historical evidence—the referent—and the actual past—the referent's referent—renders this positivist ideal philosophically untenable. Because the past as it really occurred can never be fully recovered or identical to the accounts written about it, history must be understood as a dynamic process of interpretation rather than a fixed, static structure. Historical “facts” are not pure entities existing in a vacuum; they are archaeological traces that require human judgment to be transformed into a narrative. This transformative act is where the distinction between the disciplines begins to dissolve. Historical knowledge belongs more properly to the realm of understanding than to the realm of pure reason; it is fundamentally constituted by narrative frameworks that attempt to bridge the distance between surviving signs and a lost reality. To encapsulate I’d like to quote Leopold von Ranke, an eminent German historian and a founder of modern source-based history, from his work “Histories of the Latin and Teutonic Nations” (1824)

“History has had assigned to it the task of judging the past, of instructing the present for the benefit of future ages. To such high offices, the present work does not aspire. It wants only to show what actually happened.”

Ranke's wie es eigentlich gewesen (“as it actually was”) became the motto of professional historiography, yet it conceals a fundamental paradox — the historian who claims to suppress the self must still choose which archive to enter, which documents to read, and which language to write in. The very act of narrating “what actually happened” requires the historian to decide what did not happen enough to mention, a decision that is irreducibly interpretive rather than scientific. This is precisely the illusion that postmodern historiography, and after it Indian English metafiction, sets out to expose.
The persistence of this rigid demarcation is further explained by the institutional structures that organize and reproduce academic knowledge. The modern university system is predicated on the division of labor, where departments are categorized by their perceived relationship to “truth” or “imagination.” This disciplinary soiling is not merely a bureaucratic convenience but a mechanism for establishing authority and securing research funding. History departments maintain their cultural capital by presenting their findings as objective records of the real, which in turn justifies their role in state-building and policy formation. Conversely, literature departments are often relegated to the study of the aesthetic or the subjective, reinforcing the idea that narrative is a decorative layer rather than a cognitive tool. These institutional boundaries create a feedback loop where scholars are incentivized to perform objectivity to maintain their professional standing, thereby suppressing the discursive commonalities between the two fields. The archive itself, often viewed as the ultimate site of historical truth, is an institutional construct that determines what is “saveable” and what is “forgettable,” thus pre-structuring the historian’s narrative before they even begin their work.
This epistemological vulnerability reveals that the coherence and completion found in historical accounts are products of the historian’s mind, willed and woven together rather than naturally given by the events themselves. To maintain the rigid separation between history as objective record and fiction as imaginative invention is to ignore the inherent subjectivity that defines both discourses. No history can claim pure objectivity, as the historian’s own position, prejudices, and ideological investments inevitably determine the selection and interpretation of data. The historian does not merely find a story; they create a pattern by valuing certain fragments of the past while consciously or unconsciously suppressing others. This process of “plotting” is the central structural mechanism through which the boundary between history and fiction is blurred; both disciplines employ the artifact of language and narrative frameworks to project a verbal image of reality. When a historian selects, interprets, and “serves” facts in a specific style, history itself becomes a form of discourse, making it as much a construct as any literary narrative.
The consequences of adhering to this monolithic historiography are particularly severe for the field of Indian English literary criticism and postcolonial studies. When history is treated as a single, objective “Truth,” it inevitably reinforces the monologic narratives of the dominant culture, effectively silencing alternative voices and marginalized experiences that do not leave behind “verifiable” archaeological traces. For a field concerned with the reclamation of identity and the deconstruction of colonial legacies, the positivist insistence on the “fact” acts as a gatekeeping mechanism that delegitimizes the lived experiences, oral histories, and personal memories of those written out of the official archive. This costs the field its ability to challenge the totalizing claims of the state, as it forces the literary critic to treat the novel as a mere reflection of a pre-existing historical reality rather than as an active participant in the creation of that reality. By failing to recognize the discursive nature of history, critics inadvertently uphold the very power structures they seek to interrogate, leaving the “real” in the hands of those who possess the authority to name and order the facts.
By acknowledging the “doubleness” of the postmodern mode—which involves both installing and critically questioning existing structures—it becomes possible to view fiction's engagement with the past not as an intrusion into truth but as a legitimate and necessary epistemological challenge. This shift is particularly evident in the trend of Indian English fiction since the 1980s, where writers have begun to distrust and explode officially ordered, government-sanctioned histories through subversions in narrative strategy and the use of personal memory. Once the positivist illusion is dismantled, historiography is handed over to a new theoretical description: one that recognizes history and fiction as symbiotic sites of discursive activity where the past is not merely reflected but actively re-evaluated, reconstructed, and contested. This demolition allows for the emergence of a framework where historical truth is understood as a balance of probabilities and a product of narrative negotiation, finally opening the universe of the text to the pluralities of the past.
As we move toward a synthesis of these discursive practices, the analytical focus shifts from what the past “was” to how the past is “made” within the cultural imaginary. By breaking the monopoly of the positivist record, we begin to see the archival fragment not as an end point, but as a site of potentiality where the literary imagination can intervene to recover what was silenced. This path forward suggests that the most profound historical insights may lie not in the accumulation of data, but in the critical examination of the narrative structures that give that data meaning. 
Narrative as the Shared Ground: The Structural Affinity between Historical and Fictional Discourse

The inherent affinity between history and fiction arises from the fact that both are fundamentally narrative acts which process raw facts into meaningful tales shaped by subjectivity. Rather than existing as separate epistemological entities, these two disciplines share a deep structural bond; they both rely on the narrative function to transform the discrete and discontinuous occurrence of events into a coherent representation. This structural affinity suggests that history is not an objective chronicle of time but a human construct, much like the novel, where the historian functions as a teller and the resulting history as a tale. Consequently, the traditional line between the two becomes blurred because subjectivity inevitably interferes with the treatment of historical knowledge. This interference is not a failure of the historian’s craft but an inescapable condition of human consciousness, which cannot perceive time except through the templates of story and sequence.
“The historian shares with his audience not only a notion of what constitutes an adequate explanation of historical events but also a notion of what it means for a sequence of real events to have the form and meaning of a story.”
— Hayden White, Tropics of Discourse (1978)

Historical knowledge is fundamentally constituted by narrative, an understanding that finds theoretical authority in the destabilisation of the relationship between language and reality. Following Ferdinand de Saussure’s observation that language constitutes reality rather than merely reflecting it, meaning is understood as a function of the linguistic system. This constitutive nature of language implies that the observable world must be encoded through a process of selection and sequence to become intelligible. The past, in its raw state, is an entropic mass of data points; it only attains the status of “history” when it is subjected to the grammatical and syntactical constraints of a language that is already laden with cultural value. Meaning, therefore, does not reside in the event itself but in the linguistic arrangement of that event relative to others. 
“In language, there are only differences without positive terms.” 
— Ferdinand de Saussure, Course in General Linguistics (1916)

If, as Saussure insists, linguistic signs derive meaning from difference rather than from any inherent connection to reality, then the historical “fact” — however faithfully transcribed can never be a transparent window onto the event it names. The fact is already a sign within a system, and the moment the historian arranges facts into sequence, they are performing the same grammatical and syntactical labor as the novelist. White's insight that historical narrative is emplotment, not mere transcription, thus gains a deeper linguistic foundation: it is not merely that historians choose to tell stories, but that language itself forces them to, that language is the only way for them to make sense of the past and relive it so as to comprehend the lived reality of thousands and the ongoing suffering of many. Hayden White further clarifies this by arguing that “proper history” distinguishes itself through its impulse to moralize, organizing real events to provide a coherence and closure that is not inherent in the events themselves. This process of emplotment transforms a mere list of occurrences into a dramatic arc, imposing a beginning, middle, and end upon a reality that possesses no such teleology.
Before critiquing the limitations of the positivist model that separates these fields, it is necessary to recognize the profound intellectual and ethical value inherent in the attempt to maintain a strict demarcation between history and fiction. The drive toward an objective, evidence-based historiography was born of a legitimate need to protect the past from the caprice of myth, hagiography, and state-sponsored propaganda. By insisting on a verifiable referent, the traditional historian establishes a shared reality that can be cross-examined, debated, and held accountable to material traces. This discipline provides a vital safeguard against the totalizing erasures of authoritarian regimes, which often seek to replace the messy complexity of facts with the tidy utility of nationalistic fables. To value the “fact” as a unit of truth is to respect the alterity of the past—acknowledging that people lived, suffered, and acted in ways that cannot be entirely subsumed by our contemporary imaginative needs. Without this commitment to the archival record as a grounding force, the past becomes a blank screen for the projections of the present, losing its power to challenge our current assumptions or provide a stable foundation for collective memory.
However, the persistence of the rigid separation between these disciplines is not merely a matter of intellectual preference but is deeply rooted in the institutional structures of the modern university and the state. The professionalization of history as a “scientific” endeavor served a specific disciplinary function: it allowed the academy to claim a monopoly on the “real,” thereby securing social authority and institutional funding. By categorizing history as a social science and literature as a branch of the humanities, the university system creates a structural divide that discourages the recognition of their shared discursive roots. This institutional siloing is reinforced by the requirements of the state, which relies on “objective” history to provide a stable narrative of national identity and to justify policy through a perceived continuity with the past. The archive itself, often viewed as the ultimate site of historical truth, is an institutional construct that determines what is saveable and what is forgettable, thus pre-structuring the historian’s narrative before the act of writing even begins. Consequently, the scholar who attempts to bridge this gap risks losing professional legitimacy within a system that demands a clear distinction between the verifiable “truth” of the historian and the imaginative “invention” of the novelist.
This blurring of boundaries is not reserved solely for experimental or contemporary novels but is evident in foundational historical-political documents, such as Jawaharlal Nehru’s The Discovery of India. Nehru’s project was a nationalistic endeavour designed to reclaim Indian history and heritage from colonial distortions. In this text, Nehru acknowledges that history is almost always written by the victors and conquerors, reflecting their specific viewpoints while suppressing alternative truths. By writing from a particular point in history with the ideological purpose of defending his country against colonial forces, Nehru demonstrates that even “scientific” or formal historical accounts are narrative constructions shaped by the intellectual and social currents of their period. 
“I want to tell the people of India about India. About India's past, present and future. I want them to understand the background of their country.”
— Jawaharlal Nehru, The Discovery of India (1946)
His history is a search for meaning in a state of meaninglessness, highlighting that historical documents are often searches for nationalistic or ideological order rather than objective mirrors of the past. Nehru’s work reveals that the historian does not merely discover a pre-existing nation; they narrate it into being, using the same tropes of character, conflict, and resolution that characterize the literary imagination.
The cost of maintaining this artificial polarity is particularly high for the field of Indian English literary criticism. When the discipline accepts the positivist claim that history is an objective record while fiction is a mere aesthetic diversion, it effectively neuters the political and epistemological power of the novel. By relegating the imaginative to the realm of the “unreal,” critics often overlook the ways in which fiction acts as a primary site of resistance against the monologic “History” imposed by colonial and post-colonial states. This segregation limits the critic's ability to see the novel as a sophisticated historiographical tool that can recover subaltern voices and marginalized experiences that the official archive has systematically ignored or erased. If the literary narrative is seen only as a reflection of a pre-existing historical “truth,” then the novelist’s work is reduced to a secondary commentary rather than an active intervention in the production of knowledge. This creates a critical blind spot where the specific narrative strategies used by Indian writers to contest and subvert official records are dismissed as mere stylistic flourishes rather than fundamental challenges to the way reality is constructed and maintained.
Existing approaches to historiography have long attempted to maintain a strict separation between the two disciplines by claiming objective superiority for history based on its adherence to evidence. This tradition sought to downplay the “literary” in historiography, viewing rhetoric as antithetical to historical truth and objectivity. However, by maintaining this rigid pattern of segregation, the field loses the ability to recognize that the coherence, integrity, and completion of any historical narrative are given by the historian, not by the actual events. Clinging to a positivistic model of history ignores the fact that historical deductions are often conjectural and their meanings speculative. It maintains an artificial polarity that fails to account for the ways history and literature exert a mutual influence through their discursive practices. This creates a postmodern “doubleness” in the narrative affinity between history and fiction; it is a state where the grounding of the past is established only to be critically questioned as a linguistic construction. This doubleness ensures that the narrative relationship is not accidental but constitutive of how we understand the past.
Once this structural affinity is acknowledged and the myth of objective superiority is overcome, it becomes possible to view the novel as a legitimate site for historiographical intervention. Establishing narrative as the shared ground allows for a reframing of the relationship between the two disciplines as symbiotic rather than conflictual. This epistemological shift enables the investigation to move beyond mere comparison and toward the study of forms that self-consciously exploit this overlap. By identifying historiography as a discursive site where human desire for the imaginary must contest with the imperatives of the real, we set the stage for understanding how contemporary fiction can intentionally subvert recorded truths and question the authority of official history. This shared ground provides the necessary precondition for the introduction of historiographic metafiction, a form that inhabits this narrative intersection to interrogate both the traditions of literary writing and the ideologies underlying historiography. As this inquiry moves forward, the focus will shift to how the dismantling of these binaries allows for a more pluralistic and ethical engagement with the past. By examining the gaps and silences in the archive, we can begin to see how the literary imagination does not simply fill in missing facts but fundamentally questions the criteria by which a fact is deemed worthy of memory. This opening suggests a new mode of reading where the tension between the “actual” and the “imagined” becomes the very engine of historical consciousness.
Historiographic Metafiction as Critical Concept: Hutcheon, White, and the Theoretical Vocabulary of Self-Conscious Historical Fiction

Historiographic metafiction serves as the primary analytical site where the problematized categories of historiography and the novel intersect, functioning not as a location of conflict but as a symbiotic ground for investigating the nature of historical representation. As Linda Hutcheon posits, this concept defines a specific genre of postmodern fiction that is simultaneously self-reflexive regarding its own status as a linguistic construct and paradoxically engaged with the actual events and personages of the past. This “doubleness” is the defining characteristic of the postmodern project: it installs and employs existing historical structures only to critically question the very grounds upon which those structures rest. Consequently, historiographic metafiction is not a simple anti-historical practice but a dialectical one that remains rooted in the historical while interrogating its epistemological foundations. It refuses the naive binary of either absolute fact or pure fabrication, choosing instead to inhabit the friction between the archival record and the narrative imagination. By asserting that we can only know the past through its textual remains, it forces the reader to confront the reality that historical truth is always mediated by the narrative apparatus of the present.
This theoretical practice is deeply situated within the broader intellectual shift of postmodernism, which is characterized by the rejection of totalizing “grand narratives” in favour of fragmented images and localized knowledge. In the Lyotardian sense, the postmodern condition necessitates a suspicion of monolithic historical accounts that claim to offer a singular, objective truth. By rejecting these universalizing systems, historiographic metafiction underscores the perception that history is a “structure” in which facts are conceptualized and arranged by the observer rather than being found as raw, neutral data. The field of historical knowledge is thus revealed to be a human construct, a body of knowledge that is as much a product of the historian’s imaginative faculty as it is a record of evidence. This transition from “History” with a capital 'H' to multiple, competing “histories” represents more than a stylistic preference; it is a fundamental reconfiguration of how human beings occupy time. It suggests that the past is not a destination to be reached, but an ongoing discursive site that must be constantly re-negotiated to prevent the calcification of meaning into dogma.
The operational precision of historiographic metafiction as an analytical tool is further enhanced by its recognition of the shared narrative strategies of both history and fiction. As Hayden White argues, historical narratives are essentially verbal fictions whose contents are as much invented as they are found. Both the historian and the novelist employ a poetic process of selection and sequence to weave discrete, discontinuous events into a coherent totality. However, where traditional historiography seeks to elide this process of construction to maintain an illusion of objectivity, historiographic metafiction overtly lays bare its condition of artifice. By drawing attention to its status as a text, it poses fundamental questions about the relationship between reality and the linguistic medium through which that reality is mediated. This transparency does not diminish the value of the historical event; rather, it elevates the status of the narrative form from a transparent window to a refractive lens. It acknowledges that the act of “telling” is itself an event that alters the weight and trajectory of the facts it purports to describe, thereby closing the gap between the event and its representation.
Before critiquing the limitations of traditional labels like the “political novel” or “postcolonial fiction,” one must acknowledge the indispensable pedagogical and moral clarity provided by these modes within the academic tradition. Realist historical fiction, in particular, has performed the vital task of grounding the reader in the material conditions of the past, providing a stable referent through which the horrors of colonialism or the triumphs of independence can be viscerally understood. The value of the stable political novel lies in its ability to witness: it offers a testimonial function that relies on the transparency of language to ensure that the gravity of historical injustice is not lost in a labyrinth of self-reflexive play. By maintaining a clear distinction between the observer and the observed, these traditional forms have successfully mobilized collective consciousness and fostered national identities by presenting a legible, authoritative version of the past. This adherence to the conventions of realism is often an ethical choice, intended to honor the lived reality of those whose suffering is too profound to be treated as a mere linguistic game. Without this foundational commitment to the representability of the real, the project of recovery that defines much of early postcolonial literature would lack the necessary authority to challenge colonial hegemony.
The persistence of these broad, stabilizing labels within the academy is not merely an oversight but is sustained by the institutional requirements of the university and the global publishing industry. Academic departments and curricula are frequently organized around nationalistic or chronological silos, which favor the “political novel” because it fits neatly into established historical timelines and pedagogical objectives. This disciplinary structure reproduces a preference for texts that can be taught as transparent reflections of “History,” as they allow for easier integration with social science methodologies and state-sanctioned curricula. Furthermore, the global literary market often rewards “postcolonial fiction” that adheres to recognizable realist tropes, as these are more readily consumable as authentic dispatches from the Global South. The institutionalization of the “postcolonial” as a fixed category of identity politics incentivizes a reading practice that prioritizes thematic content over structural subversion. Consequently, the critical vocabulary remains stalled in a state of descriptive summary because the professional structures of the field are built to manage historical themes rather than to interrogate the linguistic instability that historiographic metafiction reveals.
The cost of this analytical stagnation is profound for the field of Indian English literary criticism, as it reduces the subversive potential of the text to a mere illustration of pre-existing sociological data. When critics rely on the broad rubric of the “political novel” to describe works that are fundamentally interrogating the nature of truth, they inadvertently uphold the very monolithic “History” that the novels are attempting to dismantle. This results in a lopsided critical landscape where the formal innovations of writers like Salman Rushdie or Shashi Tharoor are treated as secondary to their subject matter, effectively silencing the epistemological critique inherent in their narrative structures. By failing to deploy the precision of historiographic metafiction, the field misses the opportunity to analyze how these texts do not just reflect the Indian past but actively construct and contest it. The consequence is a critical discourse that remains tethered to the state’s own definitions of history, unable to see how the mirror house of metafiction exposes the fragility of the national narrative itself. Ultimately, this analytical deficit prevents the field from fully articulating how literature can serve as a legitimate site of historical knowledge that rivals the authority of the archive.
Beyond its formal self-reflexivity, the concept carries an essential political and emancipatory dimension. It works within established literary and historical conventions specifically to subvert them, verbalizing the silenced histories of marginalized groups that have been traditionally excluded from the monologic discourse of official historiography. This subversion allows for the revelation of decentralized histories belonging to “ex-centric others,” shifting the focus from the great figures of antiquity to the perspectives of those whose lives went unrecorded. In this way, historiographic metafiction does not merely play with the past; it performs a revisionary and holistic discipline that corrects the lopsidedness of traditional, monolithic accounts. This corrective function is not about finding a “better” truth, but about expanding the discursive space to include those whose presence was erased by the necessities of the grand narrative. By doing so, it transforms the historical record from a closed vault into an open field of contestation.
To provide a fully capacious framework for reading the selected Indian English novels, historiographic metafiction must be complemented by Bakhtin’s theories of polyphony and heteroglossia. The novel is a plastic and evolving genre that takes history out of its closed, finalized form and subjects it to a process of re-evaluation. By incorporating multiple clashing voices and diverse speech types, the polyphonic novel prevents the dominance of a single, authoritative historical perspective. This dialogic approach ensures that “truth” is presented not as a finalized product but as an ongoing process of perspectives that remain open to constant reinterpretation. This interaction between voices mirrors the tension within the nation itself, where different social, religious, and linguistic groups vie for the power to narrate their own existence. The polyphonic structure thus becomes a formal equivalent to the democratic ideal, resisting the monologic pull of state history through the sheer diversity of its internal speech.
Maintaining broader labels such as the “postcolonial fiction” or the “political novel” results in a loss of analytical precision regarding the specific linguistic and structural ways in which contemporary Indian authors dismantle historical certainty. While a political novel may address historical themes, it often does so within the stable conventions of realism that leave the authority of historical discourse unchallenged. By contrast, historiographic metafiction provides a precise vocabulary to describe how texts like Midnight’s Children or The Great Indian Novel use history as a “mirror house,” reflecting different images based on the angle and standpoint of the beholder. These texts do not simply recount the events of the Partition or the Emergency; they interrogate the very possibility of recounting them “truthfully.” They force a confrontation with the fact that the nation is not a pre-existing entity found in the archive, but a discursive project that is being written and rewritten through every act of memory and storytelling.
Once this concept is operationalized, it becomes possible to move beyond a mere thematic summary of the Indian past and instead critically map the “sites of negotiation” where memory, textuality, and power intersect to produce our understanding of the nation. By viewing history not as a static record but as a discursive performance, we can finally account for the ways in which narrative choice acts as a political gesture. Once this framework is fully established, the analysis will begin to reveal how the specific subversions of chronological time in these novels serve to destabilize the linear progress of the nation-state itself. The subsequent chapters will demonstrate how these linguistic ruptures create a space where the ghostly presence of forgotten histories can finally achieve formal recognition. We shall see that the breakdown of historical certainty is not an end point of despair, but the starting point for a more pluralistic and ethical engagement with the past.
The Historical Novel's Origins and Its Epistemological Ambition: From Walter Scott to the Postcolonial Revision

The historical novel’s genealogical trajectory reveals that the form has always maintained an epistemologically competitive relationship with academic historiography rather than a merely illustrative one. While nineteenth-century positivism sought to distance history from literary devices by privileging empirical facts, this boundary-marking was inherently unstable because the facts of history are not verifiable in the scientific sense but are instead archaeological traces or remains of a past that can never be fully recovered. This conceptualization of the fact as a trace shifts the burden of proof from the object itself to the narrative that contains it, suggesting that the past does not exist until it is emplotted. Walter Scott’s reconstruction of social totalities provided a foundational model for this competition, establishing the historical novel as a genre capable of traversing the gap between the referent of historical evidence and the past as it really was. By transforming the regional novel into a romantic and historical reconstruction, Scott’s work offered an imaginative understanding of history that functioned as a chief source of cultural insight, often preceding the professionalization of history as an academic discipline. This precedence indicates that the novel did not merely follow history; it mapped the terrain of social change before formal historiography possessed the tools to codify it.
In the Indian context, this inherited form was mediated through the socio-institutional framework of colonial education, which, following the 1835 Education Resolution, aimed to create a political elite who were Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect. This class of translated men utilized the English language and its literary forms not merely to mimic Western aesthetics but to rouse a sense of nationalism, using the historical novel to integrate communities and rouse historical consciousness against colonial oppression. The condition of being a translated man created a unique discursive space where the foreignness of the genre met the local urgency of political resistance. Consequently, early Indian practitioners like Bankim Chandra Chatterjee—often termed the Walter Scott of India—adapted the alien form to an essentially Indian sensibility, marking the beginning of a tradition that used history as a discursive tool for political self-determination. Chatterjee's turn to historical fiction was not an aesthetic exercise but a fundamentally epistemological act — a claim that the Indian past was knowable, narratable, and heroic in ways that colonial historiography categorically denied. By casting the 1763 Sannyasi Rebellion as a spiritual war of national liberation, he was not merely fictionalizing a historical event but asserting the right of the colonized to produce their own historical consciousness, to produce their own narrative, to speak their stories and struggles into existence. The form borrowed from Scott was thus emptied of its Scottish content and re-filled with Bengali political urgency, demonstrating that the historical novel is always capable of betraying the ideology of its original context. This adaptation was not a passive reception but a radical appropriation, turning the tools of the colonizer toward the construction of a cohesive national identity.
Before critiquing the limitations of this traditional, nationalistic framework, it is essential to recognize the profound historical necessity and intellectual labor it represented. The use of the historical novel as a stable genre of nationalistic propaganda or social realism was a vital defensive maneuver against the epistemic violence of colonial erasure. By presenting a coherent and heroic past, writers like Chatterjee provided a demoralized populace with a sense of continuity and dignity that the colonial record systematically denied them. This mode of writing performed a crucial social function: it created a common vocabulary of resistance and a shared temporal horizon, allowing disparate groups to see themselves as part of a singular, emerging nation. To dismiss this traditional form as merely illustrative or ideologically narrow is to ignore its role as an essential site of survival. The stability of the genre at this stage was not a lack of imagination but a strategic necessity, providing the firm ground required to stand against an empire that claimed the monopoly on civilizational history.
Despite the evident successes of this nationalistic mode, the pattern of viewing the historical novel through a lens of stable, objective mimesis persists because of the institutionalized divide between the disciplines of History and Literature in the modern university. The professionalization of history as an empirical social science requires it to maintain a clear boundary from the subjective play of fiction to preserve its own claim to authority and state-level legitimacy. When historiography is institutionalized as the objective record used for state-building and policy, literature is simultaneously relegated to the aesthetic or the personal, creating a structural binary that discourages the recognition of their shared narrative roots. This disciplinary policing ensures that the historical novel is frequently taught and analyzed as a secondary ornament to the real events of history rather than as a primary epistemological challenge to them. By siloing these fields, academic structures reproduce a hierarchy where the scientific fact remains the arbiter of truth, leaving the novel to function merely as a psychological or cultural footnote to the authorized record.
The consequences of this persistent disciplinary segregation are particularly damaging for the field of Indian English literary criticism. When the field adheres to a strict separation between history as objective record and literature as imaginative play, it loses the ability to recognize how narrative constructs the very reality it claims to reflect. This rigid binary forces critics to evaluate the historical novel on its accuracy rather than its discursive power, effectively silencing the radical potential of the form. The cost of this pattern is the erasure of the history from below; by prioritizing the official master-narrative, criticism inadvertently suppresses the subtle nuances of marginalized voices, such as the experiences of peasants or the diverse bhasha traditions that do not align with the centralized national story. Consequently, the field loses the critical leverage necessary to interrogate the power relations that decide what will be remembered and what will be erased from public memory. This lack of leverage turns literary criticism into a validator of existing power structures, as it fails to account for how the novel can intentionally subvert recorded truths to reveal the violence inherent in any authorized history.
This pattern of uncritical mimesis was decisively overcome with the publication of Salman Rushdie’s Midnight’s Children in 1981, which revolutionized the Indian English novel through a deliberate turn to history and postmodern playfulness. This pivot point radicalized the competitive relationship between fiction and historiography by turning it against both colonial and nationalist master-narratives specifically. Rushdie’s intervention demonstrated that the past is not a fossilized or scientific structure but a dynamic process that must be (re)negotiated in the present. By fragmenting the narrative and foregrounding the fallibility of memory, the novel ceases to be a mirror and becomes a palimpsest, revealing the layers of construction that constitute the real. Contemporary novelists such as Rushdie, Amitav Ghosh, and Mukul Kesavan no longer view historical events as unchangeable postulates or mere context for private lives; instead, they treat the archive as a site of active excavation where the imaginary must contest with the imperatives of the real.
These writers function as excavators who use historical consciousness to uncover truths buried underneath authorized historical records, employing allegory and fragmented narratives to disrupt the colonial claim of cultural supremacy. This shift allows for the emergence of a post-postcolonial cultural zone where the novel serves as a political activity, foregrounding narratives of common individuals that were previously lost to hegemonic discourse. Once the monolithic authority of scientific history is challenged, it becomes possible for the novel to reclaim its status as a site of alternative history. In this framework, the subversion of inherited conventions transforms the historical novel into an arena where the “pastness” of the past is rendered as the “presentness” of the present, making the Indian case a profound transformation of a global tradition that now seeks to open the universe a little more.
By moving beyond the traditional view of the historical novel as a stable reflection of the past, we begin to see how the genre can function as a primary site of epistemological resistance. The dismantling of the boundary between the fact and the trace allows for a critical reappraisal of the archive not as a graveyard of truths, but as a living site of contestation. This shift signals a transition toward an analytical model where the novel does not merely document history, but actively produces a counter-memory that challenges the very foundations of official record. Once this limitation is overcome, it becomes possible to analyze how the intersection of memory and narrative in contemporary Indian fiction serves to decolonize the temporal imagination itself. We can then begin to map the specific ways in which these texts replace the monologic authority of the state with a pluralistic, palimpsest-like understanding of time and identity.
Grand Narratives and Their Discontents: Lyotard, Postcolonial Historiography, and the Politics of Pluralistic Historical Representation in India

Jean-François Lyotard’s postulation that knowledge is produced, articulated, and communicated through narratives defines the very possibilities of action within a given society. In the Indian postcolonial context, the rejection of grand narratives is not merely an abstract postmodern gesture or a philosophical preference, but a political necessity driven by the need to reclaim historical existence for those groups excluded by dominant discursive practices. Traditional historiography in India has long functioned as a totalizing force, claiming universal authority through an impersonal mode of writing that purports to be objective, neutral, and transparent. However, this supposed objectivity is often a camouflage for the subjective biases of the historian, focusing almost exclusively on the literate and the consequential—the sultans, wazirs, and chroniclers who possessed the power to inscribe themselves upon time while effectively erasing the barely discernible traces left by ordinary subjects. Once these grand narratives are recognized as ideological constructs rather than absolute truths, it becomes possible for ex-centric groups—those who are off-centre or decentred, to use their peripheral positions to challenge dominant ideologies and offer alternative, plural histories.
To understand the tenacity of these grand narratives, one must first recognize the profound intellectual and administrative utility they provided during the formative years of the postcolonial state. Far from being merely a tool of erasure, the construction of a monolithic national history serves as a vital instrument of decolonization, providing a fragmented and traumatized populace with a shared sense of purpose and a unified identity. By synthesizing disparate regional, linguistic, and caste-based experiences into a single teleological arc of “freedom,” the traditional historian offered a psychic defense against the “divide and rule” logic of the colonial past. This grand narrative performed the essential labor of state-building, creating a coherent “Indian” subject capable of self-governance in a world that had long denied that possibility. The impulse toward totality was, at its inception, an act of intellectual sovereignty; it sought to replace the colonizer’s disparaging accounts with a narrative of dignity and ancient continuity. Without this initial phase of consolidation, the very platform from which we now critique these narratives might never have been secured.
Despite the initial necessity of such consolidation, the persistence of the monolithic pattern in Indian historiography is deeply rooted in the institutional structures of the modern university and the archive. The traditional demarcation of historical study as a “scientific” discipline requires a reliance on the verifiable written record, a structural constraint that inherently privileges the state and its administrators. Because the institutional archive was organized by colonial bureaucrats to document the governable—taxable land, criminal records, and official petitions—the historian who relies solely on these sources is structurally bound to reproduce the state’s own perspective. This disciplinary dependence on the official document creates an institutional inertia where oral histories, personal memories, and the lived experiences of the non-literate are dismissed as methodologically unsound or subjective. The university department, as a site of professionalization, reinforces this by rewarding the “neutral” synthesis of archival data while treating the “ex-centric” or literary intervention as a secondary, less rigorous form of knowledge production. Consequently, the exclusion of marginalized voices is not merely a choice made by individual historians but a byproduct of an institutional architecture designed to recognize only that which has been stamped by authority.
The political urgency of contesting these grand narratives is perhaps most evident in the deconstruction of communalism, which is often framed as an ancient religious inevitability rather than a modern political instrument. A dominant grand narrative suggests that Hindu-Muslim conflict is a timeless, ancient antagonism rooted in inherent religious differences. However, the sources demonstrate that communalism was a modern phenomenon, deliberately born and promoted by British imperialist “divide and rule” policies to save dissension and maintain colonial hegemony. By treating Indians primarily as members of religious communities rather than individual citizens, the colonial state projected social and cultural variations as rigid political divisions, establishing separate electorates that institutionalized communal identities. To leave this historical grand narrative uncontested is to accept a manufactured irrationality that continues to structure modern political violence and communal polarization in independent India. Recognizing that these communal identities are not static but are functions of specific political situations allows for a critique of how elites manipulate religious sentiments to realize their own economic and cultural aspirations.
This systemic cost of maintaining a singular nationalist grand narrative is further exemplified by the historical exclusion of the Anglo-Indian community from mainstream Indian historiography and literature. For the field of Indian English literary criticism, the price of this pattern is a profound narrowing of its critical scope, resulting in a selective amnesia regarding the unique predicament of mixed-race communities and their specific sense of home and belongingness. By adhering to a binary of Indian versus European, the field loses the ability to theorize the liminal subject who occupies the doubleness of being both or neither. When Anglo-Indians are reduced to stereotypes in dominant fiction, depicted as a community of drunkards or licentious imitators the critical field fails to engage with the complex intersections of race, class, and colonial legacy. This costs the field its claim to a truly postcolonial ethics, as it continues to marginalize those who do not fit into the “authentic” nationalist categories, rendering them homeless wanderers within the very literature meant to represent the nation. The field thus remains trapped in a reactive mode, constantly defining itself against the colonial “other” while ignoring the hybrid ties that flourished in the margins of that encounter.
It is only through the act of writing back that Anglo-Indian authors like Irwin Allan Sealy contest these conventional depictions, using memory and nostalgia to create community narratives that explain their relationship to society. These ex-centric narratives serve as the Indian-textual demonstration of Lyotard's point, as they use their marginalised positions to decentre dominant discursive practices and offer pluralistic, alternative versions of the past. Similarly, Mukul Kesavan’s Looking Through Glass provides another critical case study in how fiction intervenes to challenge the totalizing narrative of the Indian National Congress during the freedom struggle. This grand nationalist narrative often presented a blindness toward the insecurities of eighty million Muslims who were rendered effectively invisible to the party of the nation. In Kesavan's work, the 1942 Quit India Resolution is not treated merely as a heroic milestone but as a potential breach of faith that glossed over the perspectives of those committed to a secular nation yet alienated by rhetorical ultimatums. By fragmenting and disseminating main-line history, such fiction offers a revival of the margins, making space for local and peripheral voices that have been silenced by homogeneous systems of power.
Once the illusion of a single, objective historical truth is overcome, it becomes possible to recognize that every individual carries their own history of India, biased and selective as it may be. This transition from monolithic grand narratives to a multiplicity of micro-narratives allows for a pluralistic historical representation where difference suggests heterogeneity and plurality rather than binary opposition. By adopting these alternative perspectives, the postcolonial subject can move beyond the tyranny of history toward a broader, more inclusive identity that recognizes the intertwined and often indistinguishable histories of the subcontinent's diverse peoples. The dismantling of these totalizing structures reveals a historiographical landscape where the “real” is no longer the exclusive property of the state or the elite chronicler. This shift gestures toward an analytical framework where the silence of the archive is no longer a dead end, but a resonant space where the literary imagination and personal memory can reconstruct the pluralities of the past. Beyond this juncture, we can begin to see how the subversion of “official” history serves not to erase the truth, but to restore its necessary complexity.
“The Subaltern, the Archive and the Limits of Documented History”
The preceding analysis of grand narratives and their discontents reveals a fundamental tension at the heart of the postcolonial historiographical project: the very instruments deployed to contest the colonial record — the archive, the footnote, the verifiable document — are themselves products of the colonial apparatus. This tension, however, runs deeper than a mere methodological paradox. It points toward a constitutive exclusion that structures the entire edifice of professional historical knowledge, one that the Subaltern Studies collective and its most consequential theorists have identified as irreducible: the systematic impossibility of the marginalized subject's self-representation within the epistemic framework of the official archive. To understand the stakes of this exclusion for Indian English fiction's engagement with the past, it is necessary to examine the theoretical contributions of Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak and Dipesh Chakrabarty, both of whom argue, in divergent but complementary ways, that the archive is not a neutral repository of traces but a culturally specific instrument of exclusion that determines, in advance, whose experience of history will be granted the dignity of the real.
Before examining the force of this critique, one must acknowledge the genuine intellectual and ethical seriousness that motivated the archival tradition these theorists challenge. The project of empirical, evidence-based history was not a conspiracy of erasure in its inception but an ambitious attempt to protect the past from the distortions of myth and hagiography, establishing a shared standard of evidence against which even the claims of the powerful could be tested. This foundational commitment to the verifiable document represented a democratic aspiration: it sought to ensure that the state could be held accountable to material traces of its own actions that the powerful could not simply narrate the past at will without fear of contradiction. The archive, in its ideal articulation, was to be a site of public accountability, a democratic institution where the records of governance, law, and administration were preserved for the scrutiny of posterity. It is only when one examines the structural conditions under which colonial and postcolonial archives were actually assembled — what they chose to record, in which language, for whose administrative purposes, and at whose expense — that the democratic promise of the archival model reveals itself to be fatally compromised by the very power structures it was meant to hold in check.
Dipesh Chakrabarty's Provincializing Europe provides the most sustained theoretical account of how this compromise operates at the level of historical methodology itself. Chakrabarty's central contention is that the discipline of professional history, as it was institutionalized in the nineteenth century, is not a universal cognitive tool but a culturally specific European practice that emerged from a particular intellectual tradition — one shaped by the Enlightenment's faith in secular, linear, and teleological time. When this practice was exported to the colonized world as the standard of rigorous historical knowledge, it did not simply introduce a neutral methodology; it implicitly required that non-Western peoples narrate their own pasts according to categories and frameworks that had been developed to describe a European experience of modernity. As Chakrabarty observes, historicism — the doctrine that all societies are moving along a single developmental trajectory, with Europe at the apex — functioned as a mechanism of temporal deferral, a way of saying “not yet” to peoples whose present circumstances did not correspond to the European model of civilization. The postcolonial historian who accepts this framework uncritically is therefore not merely choosing a methodology; they are accepting a cognitive architecture that renders much of their own society's experience of the past either invisible or legible only as a form of backwardness, superstition, or historical exception. The institutional authority of the discipline thus depends on the systematic suppression of the very heterogeneity it claims to recover.
The cost of this suppression is most acutely theorized in Gayatri Chakrabarty Spivak's foundational interrogation of the subaltern's capacity for self-representation within existing epistemological frameworks. Drawing on the Gramscian concept of the subaltern — the subordinated social groups whose interests and experiences are systematically excluded from hegemonic discourse — Spivak argues that the problem is not simply that the marginalized have been omitted from the historical record, an omission that could be remedied by more diligent archival research or a more inclusive methodology. The problem is structural and epistemological: the archive was constituted by, and continues to operate through, a set of categories and assumptions — about evidence, about agency, about what constitutes a legitimate historical source — that were designed by and for the dominant classes. When the historian, however well-intentioned, enters the archive in search of the subaltern subject, they are searching for traces within a system that was specifically organized to render those traces either illegible or absent. As Spivak observes with particular force regarding gender, the subaltern as female is placed at the most extreme margin of this double exclusion — subject not only to the colonial administration's disregard but also to the patriarchal structures within her own community that further mediated and suppressed her capacity to speak in ways the archive would recognize as speech. The silence that greets the historian who searches for the subaltern woman in the colonial record is not the silence of one who has nothing to say, but the silence produced by an entire apparatus of knowledge that was structured to ensure she could not be heard.
The institutional dimensions of this silencing are reproduced, rather than dismantled, by the postcolonial university and its disciplinary commitments. The modern Indian academy inherited the documentary protocols of colonial administration virtually intact, preserving the same hierarchies that privileged the literate, the propertied, and the politically consequential as sources of historical knowledge. Oral testimonies, ritual practices, vernacular literary traditions, and the lived knowledge embedded in craft, agriculture, and embodied labor — all of these remain, within the framework of professional historiography, methodologically suspect, requiring supplementary legitimation by a written document before they can be admitted as evidence. The effect of this methodological orthodoxy is to replicate, at the level of epistemology, the social hierarchies of the archive's original producers: the colonial administrator, the elite nationalist leader, the English-educated professional. This institutional inertia is not merely an oversight of particular historians but a structural property of a discipline whose standards of rigor were formulated in the service of governance rather than in the service of understanding. The postcolonial academy, in perpetuating these standards without critical examination, functions not as a site of decolonization but as a mechanism for laundering colonial epistemology under the banner of scholarly objectivity.
It is precisely this epistemological violence — the violence of the archive's constitutive silence — that the works of contemporary Indian English fiction are most urgently attempting to redress. When Amitav Ghosh's narrator in The Shadow Lines registers his horror at the official erasure of the 1964 communal riots from “responsible” public discourse, he is not merely lamenting a gap in the historical record; he is dramatizing the precise operation that Spivak and Chakrabarty theorize: the production of historical non-existence for those whose suffering does not serve the nation's need for a coherent story. When Salman Rushdie's Saleem Sinai claims that his bodily memory constitutes a more truthful archive than the state's official chronology, he is performing the subaltern historian's gesture that Chakrabarty calls the “provincializing” of European epistemic authority — insisting that the bodily, the affective, and the partial constitute legitimate modes of historical knowledge that cannot be assimilated into the master narrative without remainder. When Irwin Allan Sealy's Anglo-Indian characters narrate their community's existence through nostalgia, domestic ritual, and the memory of specific tastes and landscapes, they are preserving precisely those traces of experience that the archive, organized around the legally and administratively consequential, was structurally forbidden from recording. In each case, the novel does not merely supplement the archive with additional data; it contests the archive's own criteria for admissibility, insisting that the silences of the documented record are not epistemological dead ends but resonant spaces where the literary imagination can perform the most urgent and ethically necessary work of historical recovery.
This contestation carries consequences that extend beyond the literary into the political. If the archive's silence regarding the subaltern is structural rather than accidental, then the project of recovering subaltern history cannot be completed by accumulating more documents; it requires a fundamental reconceptualization of what counts as historical evidence in the first place. The novel, with its capacity to render the interior landscape of consciousness, the texture of affective experience, and the logic of marginalized communities' self-understanding, offers precisely this reconceptualization. By asserting that the imaginative reconstruction of an undocumented life is a form of historical knowledge rather than a departure from it, historiographic metafiction challenges the epistemological foundations of the discipline at its deepest level. It does not argue that fiction is “truer” than the archive; it argues that the distinction between the documented and the imagined, upon which the archive's authority rests, is itself an ideological construction that has served the interests of the historically powerful. This argument, as Spivak and Chakrabarty demonstrate through different routes, is not a postmodern indulgence but a political necessity — the necessary first step in any genuine project of historical justice for those who were written out of the record before they were given the chance to write themselves in.
Once the subaltern's constitutive exclusion from the archive is fully recognized, it becomes possible to reframe the entire project of Indian English fiction's engagement with history not as a playful postmodern diversion from the real business of documentation, but as a sophisticated epistemological intervention in the politics of historical knowledge. The literary text, in this framework, is not a secondary reflection of a history that already exists elsewhere; it is a primary site where alternative evidential protocols are being developed, tested, and legitimated against the monopoly of the documented record. This recognition provides the theoretical ground for understanding why the formal choices of writers like Rushdie, Ghosh, Kesavan, and Sealy — their disruptions of linear chronology, their foregrounding of unreliable narrators, their embrace of myth and bodily memory as historical sources — are not merely aesthetic preferences but epistemic commitments. Each formal subversion enacts, at the level of narrative structure, the same argument that Spivak makes at the level of theory: that the criteria by which history distinguishes the real from the imagined, the knowable from the unverifiable, and the consequential from the forgotten are not universal truths but political choices, and that those choices have always already determined whose lives will be granted the dignity of historical existence.
Memory, Subjectivity, and the Archive: How Individual Recall Competes With Documented History as an Epistemological Resource

The prevailing historiographical model often views individual recall as a subordinate or unreliable witness to the documented archive, yet contemporary Indian English fiction posits memory as a rival epistemological mode that captures the “authentic taste of truth” frequently suppressed by official narratives. While traditional history seeks a scientific status through unobtrusive objectivity, factual documentation, and the extraction of general truths, these novels suggest that such an archive is often an ideological construct that serves the interests of power. By maintaining a rigid hierarchy between the scientific record and fictive recollection, the field loses the nuances of lived experience and the internalized pasts that constitute the “real world” of ordinary people, Once this binary is overcome, it becomes possible to recognize memory—personal, communal, and nostalgic as a sophisticated counter-archive that performs essential work in identity construction and historical witnessing,.
To appreciate the necessity of this counter-archive, one must first acknowledge the immense structural and ethical utility of the traditional archival model it seeks to supplement. The pursuit of “unobtrusive objectivity” was not a mere stylistic whim but a monumental intellectual project aimed at establishing a verifiable, shared reality that could transcend the vagaries of individual bias and the distortions of hagiography. By privileging the documented fact, historiography provided a rigorous methodology for adjudicating conflicting claims, ensuring that the past was not merely a canvas for contemporary projection but a territory governed by empirical constraints. This “scientific” status offered a bulwark against the totalizing myths of pre-modern chroniclers, creating a stable foundation for the nation-state and providing a language of evidence that allowed for the cross-examination of power. The archive, in its ideal form, functions as a site of public accountability where the “consequential” is preserved to ensure that the mechanisms of governance and the trajectories of major events remain transparent to future generations. Without this foundational commitment to a factual, extractable truth, the study of the past would risk collapsing into a purely solipsistic exercise where the distinction between what occurred and what is desired entirely evaporates.
Yet, the persistence of this rigid hierarchy—where memory is dismissed as mere subjectivity while the document is hallowed as a fact is reproduced through the specific institutional conditions of the postcolonial academic and state apparatus. The archive is not a neutral repository but a bureaucratic instrument; its very structure is determined by what the state deems “record-worthy,” a process that systematically excludes the ephemeral, the emotional, and the domestic. Disciplinary history maintains its authority by mirroring the state’s own requirements for legible data, rewarding the historian who utilizes “literate” sources while marginalizing those who turn to the “vagaries of memory.” This institutionalization of the archive ensures that the only pasts that remain visible are those that left a paper trail in the halls of power, thereby reinforcing a cycle where the absence of documentation is equated with an absence of historical reality. The academy, by tethering its standards of “scientific” validity to these state-curated collections, inadvertently functions as an amnesiac machine that treats the silences of the archive not as a loss to be recovered, but as the boundary of the knowable.
The consequences of this institutionalized amnesia are particularly devastating for the field of postcolonial studies and Indian English literary criticism. By adhering to a model that prioritizes “nationalistic memory” and political movements over local trauma, the field effectively participates in the second-order erasure of the subaltern subject. If the “authentic taste of truth” is only permitted to exist within the margins of the fictive, then the lived experience of the majority of the population remains historically “unreal,” trapped in a state of epistemological illegitimacy. This costs the field its critical edge; it becomes unable to theorize the profound psychological and communal shifts that occur beneath the surface of official history. When we ignore the “internalized” pasts of ordinary people, we reduce the postcolonial condition to a series of high-level political transitions, missing the “state of mind” that actually constitutes the national identity. The field thus risks becoming a mere commentary on state-sanctioned events rather than a radical investigation into how a person survives and (re)claims their place in time despite the erasures of the documented record.
For example the India-Pakistan partition of 1947 is the single largest upheaval in the subcontinent's modern history and the records of it read as if written by people with stone cold hearts, a blind eye and a numb ear to the sufferings of displaced communities and lives lost.
“Partition is a wound that has never healed. The archives show the negotiations; they do not show the screams.”
— Urvashi Butalia, The Other Side of Silence (1998)

Urvashi Butalia's oral history project, which recovered the testimonies of women and children displaced by Partition, demonstrated that the most consequential events of the subcontinent's modern history were precisely those that the official archive was structurally least equipped to hold. The state's records documented borders drawn, properties transferred, and populations counted; they did not and structurally could not document the interior landscapes of grief, displacement, and divided loyalty that constituted the “real” experience of 1947 for the fifteen million people who crossed those new lines. They weren’t equipped to hold the cries of children who lost their father, wives who lost their husband, families who lost their ancestral home, property, their ancestral belongings feeling as if severed from the very ancestors that fought for peace and their belongingness to this piece of land. The neat history records in actuality are soaked in blood, the borders documented are scars on bodies, the properties transferred is baggage of grief passed on and the populations counted is trauma that will wrap itself around people’s lives and manifest in their everyday reality, haunting and clouding perceptions of the present and of the generations to come. So when Ghosh's narrator in The Shadow Lines refuses to locate truth in the documented record, he is performing the same epistemological maneuver as Butalia: turning away from the archive toward the witness, recognizing that the most unimpeachable historical resource is sometimes the trembling voice that the document was designed to silence.
In Amitav Ghosh’s The Shadow Lines, memory is not merely a narrative device but the primary structure through which the history of the nation is reclaimed. The narrator identifies a profound silence in the official record regarding the communal riots of 1964, which have been vanished from the “collective imagination” of responsible opinion and the history books. This erasure forces the individual to rely on his own vividly remembered experience, asserting that his internal past is a more truthful archive than the selective “nationalistic memory” that prioritize wars and political movements over lived trauma. By prioritizing individual perceptions of reality, Ghosh rectifies the status of history as an objective record, presenting it instead as a dynamic relation of the subject to their own past. This recalibration suggests that the most consequential events are often those that the archive is least equipped to hold—the “local trauma” that leaves no monument, no paper trail in power halls but persists in the sensory recall of the witness.
Salman Rushdie further develops this epistemological challenge through the metaphor of “chutnification,” where history is “pickled” to preserve time and experience. While the pickling process inevitably involves a certain alteration and selective distortion, it is presented as a means to give raw events shape, form, and meaning. Rushdie argues for “memory’s truth,” which may select, eliminate, and exaggerate, but ultimately creates a coherent version of events that is more reliable to the individual than any external account as it is laced with emotions and experienced reality. This “pickles' version of history” serves as a defiant alternative to the amnesiac nation and the official, politicians' versions of truth, demonstrating that even partial memory can provide a more honest epistemological position than the false completeness of documented historiography. The “alteration” inherent in memory is here reframed not as a flaw, but as the necessary interpretive labor required to make a chaotic past coherent and comprehensible to the present self.
This shift from the documented to the remembered is particularly acute for the Anglo-Indian community, as explored in the works of Irwin Allan Sealy, where memory functions as an “ongoing constructive process” used to create imaginary homelands in the absence of a fixed geographical state. For this displaced minority, India as a homeland is produced through the experience of nostalgia and the narration of personal associations, creating a state of mind- the only safe, non-questioning and accepting one for them, which allows the community to (re)claim its place in history. Memory here operates as a channel of communication between the past and the present, enabling the formation of a collective identity that historical records, often focused on the “literate and the consequential,” systematically choose to ignore. Sealy’s work illustrates that for marginalized groups, the imaginary is not a retreat from the real, but the very mechanism through which a “real world” is constructed and inhabited when the official geography and documented history offers no room.
The apparent opposition between memory’s subjectivity and history’s supposed objectivity dissolves when one recognizes that both are narrativized through the reconstruction of “narrative substances”. Traditional historiography often camouflages the historian’s “I” behind third-person narration to imply an impartial, non-partisan view, yet this is a deliberate attempt to suppress the subjective nature of historical judgments. Novels like Shashi Tharoor’s The Great Indian Novel explicitly disrupt this pretense by using interlocutors like Ganapathi to foreground the “vagaries of memory” and the presence of the reader within the text. These “meta-autobiographies” acknowledge that the past exists only in memories and the words that strive to encapsulate them, making the process of writing a biography or a history a meta discursive operation rather than an objective transcription. Historical truth is thus contextual and radical, as the textual nature of our knowledge indicates there is no reality outside the representation.
By integrating historical discourse with personalized narration, these texts demonstrate that the subject in history is always a subject of history, perpetually “handcuffed” to a past that is being reconstructed in the present. This recognition allows the novelist to use memory as a “positive alternative” that retains the traces of lived experience, ultimately offering a more comprehensive and comprehensible form to the chaos of national events. Once memory is fully integrated into the historiographical framework, it becomes possible to map the subterranean currents of desire, fear, and belonging that actually drive the consequential events of the surface. By moving beyond the binary of fact and fiction, we open an analytical space where the silences of the archive become as loud and informative as its spoken records. This shift signals a move toward a more ethical historiography that views the “imaginary homeland” not as a fabrication, but as the most resilient form of historical truth.
History vs. Fiction: A False Binary?
The question of whether history and fiction are truly distinct epistemological categories or whether the boundary between them is a disciplinary convenience masquerading as an ontological fact sits at the center of the theoretical concerns this chapter has been developing. To pose it as a binary at all is already to accept a framework that the preceding analysis has worked systematically to unsettle. Yet the question cannot be dissolved simply by asserting that the two are “really the same,” for such a gesture would be its own form of intellectual violence, erasing real and consequential differences in the name of a theoretical elegance that the evidence does not fully support. The most productive position, therefore, is neither to maintain the binary nor to collapse it, but to hold it open — to examine, with equal rigor, both the profound structural affinities that challenge the separation and the genuine distinctions that make it difficult to abandon entirely. What emerges from this double examination is not a verdict but a provocation: that the binary of history versus fiction is less a description of two different relationships to truth than a description of two different institutional contracts with the reader, each of which conceals, in its own characteristic way, the interpretive labor required to produce the version of reality it presents.
The most fundamental affinity between the two discourses lies in their shared and inescapable dependence on narrative. Neither the historian nor the novelist is confronted with a reality that is already organized into a story; both must impose or, to use Hayden White's more precise term, emplot, a structure of beginning, middle, and end upon a raw material that in itself possesses no such teleology. The historian who narrates the Indian Independence movement as a story of heroic national awakening culminating in the triumph of 1947 is making the same fundamental formal choice as the novelist who structures a bildungsroman around a protagonist's journey from ignorance to self-knowledge: both are selecting a shape, a rhythm, and a point of closure that the events themselves did not possess until the narrator provided them. The Partition and its attendant violence could equally have been the culmination of that same story, transforming it from a narrative of liberation into a tragedy of division. That most mainstream historiography chose the former and most literary responses to Partition chose the latter is not a reflection of the events but a reflection of the different ideological imperatives governing the two practices at the moment of narration. Both choices are narrative; neither is transparent. The shared grammar of storytelling — character, conflict, causation, resolution; ensures that every historical account, however rigorously sourced, participates in the same fundamental act of world-making that defines the literary imagination.
Closely allied to this narrative dependency is the equally inescapable necessity of selection. No history can tell everything; no novel can represent the full texture of the reality it invokes. Both practices are constituted by exclusion as much as by inclusion, and both must suppress the majority of available material to construct a coherent account of any portion of it. The historian's selection, however, is governed by the institutional protocols of the archive — which sources count as evidence, which events are deemed consequential enough to merit documentation, which voices are admitted as reliable witnesses; protocols that, as the preceding section has argued, are themselves ideologically freighted rather than neutrally objective. The novelist's selection is governed by the requirements of narrative coherence, emotional plausibility, and the demands of the form being employed. In both cases, what is left out is as ideologically significant as what is included. Nehru's The Discovery of India selects a version of Indian civilization that serves the requirements of anti-colonial nationalism; it excludes, among other things, the caste hierarchies and internal oppressions that complicated the picture of a unified, ancient, and essentially democratic Indian spirit. This selection is not dishonest in a simple sense; it is purposeful, which is to say, it is precisely as purposeful as the novelist who foregrounds certain characters while consigning others to the margins of the narrative. The difference is that the historian's selection is presented as the result of following the evidence, while the novelist's selection is acknowledged as the product of creative judgment. Yet if the evidence itself is, as Chakrabarty demonstrates, a pre-selected body of material organized by the priorities of state and administration, then the historian's claim to be “following” rather than “choosing” is itself a narrative: a story about the process of historical narration that the process itself does not support.
The ideological dimension of both practices is perhaps the most consequential affinity and the most systematically denied. Every historical account, regardless of how scrupulously it adheres to its sources, reflects the intellectual and political horizon of the moment in which it is written. The categories through which the historian organizes the past — nation, class, religion, civilization, progress are not found in the archive but brought to it, and they carry with them the valuations and assumptions of their cultural moment. The British colonial historiography of India, which narrated the subcontinent's pre-colonial past as a period of despotism and stagnation from which British administration rescued it, was not lying in the sense of fabricating evidence; it was selecting and organizing genuine evidence through categories that were ideological constructs rather than neutral descriptors. The nationalist counter-history that replaced it was equally ideological, selecting a different set of categories — “ancient wisdom,” “spiritual civilization,” “freedom struggle”, that served the requirements of decolonization rather than the requirements of the colonial state. Neither was more “objective” in any meaningful sense; each was ideologically coherent in relation to the political project it served. The novel operates under no institutional obligation to deny this ideological positioning; indeed, the greatest works of historiographic metafiction — Midnight's Children, The Great Indian Novel, The Shadow Lines to name a few, derive much of their critical force from the ostentatious display of their own ideological situatedness, their refusal to pretend that the narrator stands outside the history being narrated. In this respect, the novel's acknowledged partiality may be said to be more epistemologically honest than the historian's performed objectivity, but this is not the same as saying the novel is more truthful only that it is more candid about the conditions of its own production.
To concede all of this, however, is not to conclude that history and fiction are indistinguishable, and intellectual honesty requires that the case for their genuine difference be made with equal care. The most significant distinction between the two lies not in their relationship to narrative or selection where, as the preceding analysis demonstrates, the similarities are profound but in their relationship to verification and their respective contractual obligations to the reader. The historian operates under a disciplinary obligation to provide a trail of evidence that, in principle, any sufficiently trained reader could follow, interrogate, and contest. The footnote, the bibliography, the citation, these are not merely scholarly conventions; they are the formal mechanisms through which historical claims are made accountable to a community of knowledge. When a historian asserts that the Bengal Famine of 1943 was exacerbated by deliberate policy decisions, that claim is answerable to evidence in a way that can be publicly adjudicated. A novelist who makes the same assertion within a fictional narrative operates under no such obligation; the claim cannot be verified by reference to the text itself, and the reader has no formal recourse if the historical imagination proves inaccurate. This asymmetry of accountability is not trivial; it is the foundation of history's social function as a mechanism for holding power responsible to the record of its own actions. To collapse the distinction entirely is to risk losing the very tool by which the manipulations of state propaganda and historical revisionism can be publicly contested.
There is, moreover, a difference in the nature of the claims each discourse makes about its relationship to the real. The novel, even when it incorporates historical figures and documented events, operates within what the philosopher John Searle has called a system of “fictional assertions” — speech acts that are not intended as first-person commitments to the truth of what is being stated, and that the reader understands as such. When Rushdie writes that Saleem Sinai was born at the midnight of Independence, no reader takes this as a claim requiring verification against the birth registers of Bombay's hospitals. When a historian writes that Nehru delivered his “Tryst with Destiny” speech on the night of August 14th, 1947, the claim is of an entirely different ontological order: it is a sincere assertion, answerable to evidence, and intended as a contribution to a shared reality that exists independently of the text. The fictional assertion and the historical assertion are, in this sense, performing different illocutionary acts, and conflating them risks not only a theoretical confusion but a practical one i.e. the evacuation of the evidentiary standards that allow democratic societies to distinguish between documented history and politically motivated myth-making. In an age when historical revisionism and the manipulation of the past for nationalist purposes are increasingly consequential political forces, the distinction between the imagined and the verified retains a social urgency that purely theoretical arguments for their equivalence cannot dissolve.
What the preceding analysis ultimately suggests, therefore, is not that history and fiction are the same, but that the traditional binary which opposes them as fact to invention, real to imagined, objective to subjective, is inadequate to describe the actual epistemological complexity of either practice. Both are narrative; both are selective; both are ideological and these affinities are too profound and too structurally grounded to be dismissed as superficial resemblances. But the historian's accountability to an external evidential standard and the novelist's freedom from that accountability represent a genuine difference in the social function and epistemological contract of each discourse, a difference that has real consequences for how knowledge claims are made, tested, and contested in public life. The most intellectually honest position, and the one that the literary texts under examination in this study consistently model, is to hold both truths simultaneously: to insist on the narrative, selective, and ideological construction of all historical knowledge, while refusing the comfortable conclusion that this construction licenses a wholesale equivalence of the documented and the invented. 
The binary may be false as an absolute epistemological division, but the questions it raises about evidence, accountability, and the social responsibilities of representation remain as urgent as ever. It is precisely in the friction between these two recognitions that the boundary is constructed, and yet that boundaries matter, that the most searching and ethically responsible engagements with the past, whether historical or fictional, are produced.
